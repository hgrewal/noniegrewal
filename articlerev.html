What Drives Women Out of Computer Science &
What Needs To Be Done About It.

In his artile, What Has Driven Women Out of Computer Science, Randall Ross discusses
the relavant factors that has caused a decrease of women in Computer Science and Technology.
I review his dissertation and give my own on what I have experience and believe what should be
done to not only recruit, but retain more women in Computer Science



         As a woman in computing I have a unique opinion on women in computing, or more over the lack of women in computing. First of all, I will admit that it makes me sad to see so few women in a career that I feel has so much potential. I believe that women are just as likely to enjoy and succeed in computing as men are. That being said, where my opinion does differ from many other popular beliefs is the women suffer in Computer Science due to little support and outright oppression.
         I understand that there is a great disparity in the number of women in the technical field, and men. But I do not believe that this creates a disadvantage for the women that are already in Computer Science. Take for examples an experience that I had the second year of my undergraduate studies.
         While working on a group project for an organization I encountered a peculiar situation. Out of the fifteen or so people in the room, I was the only woman. The group leader began to talk about what we needed to do in order to prepare to work on the project - this included downloading appropriate software and prepping our working environments. Once he explained what was to be done, he immediately walked over to me and asked if I needed help. He then proceeded to take my laptop and help me install the software.
         I was obviously stunned and did not know what to do. I did not need help. I never asked for help. It had been established that no one else in the room had worked with the tools we were expected to download either - we were all in the same boat. Why did the he assume that I needed help?
         From my experience I have learnt that not only are women not expected to pursue a Computer Science career, as illustrated by Randall Stross in his article, but they are assumed to not belong either. And yes I can see how this story is coming to a conclusion that will directly contrast my initial claim - that women are not oppressed in Computer Science - but to me it is the exact opposite.
         I remember I immediately became incredibly offended. A part of me wanted to walk out and never come back to what I thought of then as a misogynistic environment. But once I had cooled down, I realized that me quitting would be directly encouraging the belief that women do not belong or are oppressed in Computer Science. This is what prompted me to continue with the project, and not only that but to work ten times harder than I had planned on. I wanted to prove my legitimacy, not to show the rest of the members of the project, but to prove to myself that I am just as capable.
         In his article Stross defines the many attributes that drive women out of Computer Science. He stresses the fact that not only is the number of women lacking in Computer Science, but they are decreasing over the years. In the last twenty-five years the number of women in computer science has dropped from 40% to less than 10%. A tribulation that is unique to Computer Science when comparing to other Science and Engineering majors, in which the number of women has and is increasing.
         Ross reveals some of the factors that may be attributed to the decrease of women. He reports that a reason why more women were interested in Computer Science in the past was because the present-day stereotypes of programming did not exist at the time. This is the "nerd" or "geek" profile given to programmers. More over the subculture of action video games was not present at the time, another implication that computers are for men. Ross believes that it is this stigma that repels women from joining Computer Science.
         Although I understand Ross's dissertation, and to a large extent agree with it; I feel that the greater issue is that women are defined to be unbelonging in Computer Science. And furthermore, this is a perception that is often times shared by women themselves. Ross further suggests this can be solved by implementing a sub-culture that relates women's interests with coding directly. In addition to his suggestion, I believe that we should be focusing on breaking the theoretical barriers that women feel prohibit them from succeeding in Computer Science, and instead building the bridges that remove the concept of gender from software engineering altogether.
         That is that men and women should focus on treating one another equally with in the major. Women should not be seen as the lesser, or the minority. Instead there should be direct measures taken that disregard the gender disparity altogether. Stop talking about how it feels to be a woman in technology, and instead be a professional technologist.
         Going back to my experience, it was not until I got over my initial feelings of anger and anguish, that I realized the group leader was honestly trying to be nice. I do not think he had a negative motive, but instead was ignorant to the fact that I did not need additional help because I am a woman. It was then that I was able to "ignore" his actions, my so-called oppression, and work around it (or with it depending on how you see it) and just get my part of the project done in an efficient and professional manner.
         Therefore, I do agree with Ross, that another important (more important in my opinion) step needed to be taken is the education of Computer Science and its potential, which does not solely lie in gaming, is necessary at an early age. This way we can break the negative perceptions young men and women may have about programming allowing them a fair opportunity to join Computer Science.
